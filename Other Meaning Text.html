Malcolm, Media, and Identity

Malcolm X once said, “The media’s the most powerful entity on earth. They have the power to make the innocent guilty and to make the guilty innocent.” Given that you have now read some both Dr. King and Malcolm X's self-portrayals in their own words, reflect on the importance of telling one's own story. What have you learned additionally about each man through his own words? Why is it important that you tell your own story outside of stereotypes and popular media?

Malcolm X and the Media Fracture

Malcolm X’s relationship with media reveals a deeper truth about identity, power, and perception. His words are just raw, unfiltered, and rooted in justice, helding potential to influence millions of people. Yet mainstream outlets often distorted those words, framing him as a threat rather than a thinker. This wasn’t an misrepresentation; it was a calculated manipulation that reflected broader systems of racism and control. In contrast to Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. on the other hand, was often portrayed as the peaceful, palatable face of the civil rights movement; his calls for nonviolence and integration widely broadcasted and embraced by white audiences. But even that portrayal was selective, often omitting his critiques of capitalism, militarism, and systemic inequality. Both men understood that mythology and media could work together to reshape public perception, turning truth into controversy and resistance into danger. Their legacies remind us that even the most honest voices can be twisted when filtered through biased lenses.

That’s why self-authorship is important. In a world where stereotypes, lies, and hypocrisy dominate the narrative, telling your own story becomes a form of resistance. Independent publishing, masses media, and direct evidence allow individuals to reclaim their justice and present it with coherence and intention. Malcolm X didn’t just speak; he documented, corrected, and clarified. Dr. King, too, used his writings and speeches to push beyond the cleaned image, revealing a radical vision for truth within. Both men knew that the most accurate portrayal of a person comes internal, not from external systems built to distort. Their lives stand as blueprints for how identity, media, and how truth intersect, and why our rights must be protected, especially when it challenges the status quo. Through self-authorship, we don’t just tell our stories; we define them, on our own terms.
